## Banning Due to LGBTQIA+ Themes and Content

<sup>Rudy, Abby, Tanay, Caroline</sup>

### Historical Context

LGBTQ themes in literature have historically faced significant challenges and objections with writers facing legal restrictions, book bans, and persecution. Despite these hurdles, LGBTQ literature can be traced to thousands of years ago in ancient times. Greek poets Sappho and Theocritus celebrated homoeroticism as did later Roman poets Catullus and Martial. Although the idea of LGBTQ literature has been around for awhile, the acronym "LGBTQ" is a much more recent and Western idea.

One more modern and important literary figure who focuses on LGBTQ and gender issues was Oscar Wilde (1854–1900). Much of his work critiques societal norms regarding sexuality, gender, and more specifically his experience as an isolated gay man. His work in the 19th century along with other authors' works, paved the way for more LGBTQ awareness among readers. Many notable authors who questioned society and brought to light the LGBTQ experience were women and their contributions tend to go unnoticed. Some of these authors include Gertrude Stein, Virginia Woolf, and Leslie Feinberg.

Throughout the 20th Century, LGBTQ literature significantly increased creating a more socially accepting environment for queer peoples. Authors such as James Baldwin, Audre Lorde, and Truman Capote achieved acclaimed success with their works and they also paved the way for more inclusion for LGBTQ individuals. In the 21st century, LGBTQ literature has exploded and the acceptance of same-sex couples has become more and more normal. Especially with legislative wins for LGBTQ individuals to legally have same-sex marriage. Overall, LGBTQ literature throughout history has brought awareness to the queer experience and has made sexuality a much more accepted topic in society.

### Book Examples

A few books that have been banned in different states due to their LGBTQ and identity content include [*All Boys Aren't Blue*](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/07group7.html) by George Johnson, [*They Both Die at the End*](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/08group3.html) by Adam Silvera, and [*Cinderella is Dead*](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/02group2.html) by Kalynn Bayron.

- **All Boys Aren't Blue** — A Young Adult (YA) memoir of personal essays that portrays the author's experiences growing up as Black and queer in America. Banned in 2022 in Florida.
- **They Both Die at the End** — A Young Adult fictional book where two queer strangers connect and receive news they only have 24 hours to live. Banned in 2022 in Texas.
- **Cinderella is Dead** — A Young Adult dystopian novel that portrays a girl who rebels against the patriarchy and falls in love with her best friend. Banned in 2022 in Florida.

The similarity between all these bans is that all of these books are for young adults — people who are still exploring their identity. There also seems to be a trend in date and location: Florida is the primary state for book bans and Texas comes second, and these have surged in recent years ("Book Bans"). Many of these books were popular on BookTok and are enjoyed by many; however, the bans on these books restrict their content to many people. These bans echo the issue of self-exploration and expression as many are being denied access to books that they may resonate with or enjoy. The trend of bans continues and now more and more LGBTQ and identity books are being restricted.

### Context of Modern Book Bans

More than 3,360 books were banned during the 2022–2023 period, and these book bans disproportionately target the LGBTQ group. They are often accused as "pornographic" or "promoting a gay agenda." Removing these books isolates LGBTQ youth, depriving them from understanding their identities and can lead to depression.

Additionally, the American Library Association reported 1,269 attempts to ban or restrict books in 2022, which was double the previous record and nearly half targeted LGBTQ titles. Recently, LGBTQ books are still at the center of book banning across America, showing how queer stories continue to be targeted. Groups like PEN America and the American Library Association have documented thousands of book removals. The queer books banned were justified with the claim that they are "protecting the children."

### Conclusion

Banning LGBTQ and identity books is banning self-expression and self-exploration. In 2023 the most targeted book was *Gender Queer* by Maia Kobabe. It is clear that this theme is widely targeted and continues to be targeted in the present day.
