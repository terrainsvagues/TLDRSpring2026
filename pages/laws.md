## The Legal and Educational Context Behind Book Bans

<sup>Cooper, Elizabeth, Riya, Eric</sup>

### Historical Foundations of Legal Censorship

One consistent theme in book bannings within the United States over the last 400 years is the struggle between legislation protecting the rights of freedom of speech and the press in conflict with legislation meant to preserve social, moral, and legal hierarchies. Throughout each period of American history, moralist movements sought to preserve the existing social order by stifling dangerous ideas through legal means. This began even in the pre-Independence era, when colonial governments such as the Massachusetts Bay Colony sought to restrict ideas that challenged their puritanical religious and gubernatorial authority. This was reflected by the 1637 banning of *New English Canaan*. This moralist impulse to ban literature that challenged the existing social fabric was reflected in the 18th and 19th century, where religious movements seeking to align American society more closely with religious ideals attempted to remove difficult ideas through legislation, most significantly with the 1873 Comstock Act. This act sought to enforce a moral purity within the public sphere, criminalizing "obscene," sexually explicit materials. On more ideological grounds, during the Communist red scare of the 1960s, legislation was used to crack down on literature once more; this time under the auspices of protecting democratic, capitalist ideals. That leads us into the modern era of book bannings, which has been legislated under similarly knee-jerk reactionary policies dedicated to preserving existing ideas of traditional hierarchies and social structures under fire from a rapidly changing national identity.

### Legal Precedents and State-Level Action

Book banning in the US isn't new, but the institutionalized role of law and policy in censorship has grown dramatically in the 21st century, particularly in regards to LGBTQ+, race, and sexual content. Early American book censorship stretches back centuries. For example, there was a ban in 1637 in Massachusetts on Thomas Morton's *New English Canaan*. However, modern legal arguments about book removal have often involved questions of constitutional rights and local authority. One important legal benchmark in book censorship is the *Island Trees School District v. Pico*-era cases, where courts held that school boards cannot remove books simply because they disagree with the ideas inside of them, emphasizing that the First Amendment protects access to diverse information. Yet recent state laws have tested these precedents. Since 2021, many states have passed laws giving parents or officials formal tools to challenge and remove books, blurring the line between individual complaints and policy-driven censorship. For example, states like Florida have passed broad education statutes linked to book removals, and others like Indiana have enacted laws allowing books deemed "harmful to minors" to be more easily contested.

### The Shift from Educators to Parents

The landscape of this book censorship has shifted from isolated local complaints to a coordinated movement at the level of state legislation. Notably, there has been an unprecedented volume of accumulated banned books in the U.S., especially targeting on the premise of the potential harm in students' education and learning opportunities. To further examine why there has been such an increase in caution and filtration of what students may learn, we must look at the shift of authority from the board of education to the parents. Historically, the BOE has used professional librarians and educators to curate materials for a community. However, there have been emerging laws that are actively mitigating this buffer. In the 2025 landmark case [*Mahmoud v. Taylor*](https://www.scstatehouse.gov/sess126_2025-2026/bills/243.htm#:~:text=%28D%29%20If%20the%20board%27s%20action,any%20violation%20of%20this%20article.) the Supreme Court ruled that parents have a constitutional right to opt their children out of instruction involving books they find religiously or morally objectionable (specifically targeting LGBTQ+ themes). This forces schools to create curricula that simply removes any "controversial" books to fully encompass all students' learning opportunities and make it as parallel as possible. Additionally, many state laws such as those from South Carolina and Florida now give parents the right to sue a school district directly if they feel their "parental rights" have been infringed by the board not actively complying with their complaints. The threat of expensive litigation often compels boards to side with the parents rather than the educational experts. With the overflow of information, whether true or false, right or wrong, has been near to impossible in today's world, which somewhat explains the increased litigation over what our future generations will learn through books. The parents are using these laws as a defense mechanism against the digital world they can no longer control — using the physical school library as the last place where they can exercise filtration.
