## Banning Due to Anthropomorphic Animals

<sup>Mahammad, Adela, Qianru, Josh</sup>

Many books with anthropomorphic animals are often banned for two major reasons. One reason is that projecting human traits to animals is considered "heresy" to orthodox principles and/or religion. One example of this is [*Alice in Wonderland*](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/05group9.html) by Lewis Carroll, banned in Hunan, China in 1931. This book was unbanned afterward. The other is because authors often use them as metaphors to comment on conflicts and events that occurred in the world. A classic example of this is [*Animal Farm*](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/01group9.html) by George Orwell, which was banned in the Soviet Union in 1945 for satirizing communism, and banned in Wisconsin in 2002 for involving political content.

Historically, anthropomorphic storytelling dates back to ancient fables such as Aesop's Fables, and animals have been used in literature from the 6th century BC, often serving as a representation of human values, moral lessons, and relatable protagonists. Since then, animals have been seen in roles of allegory, satire, and fables that challenge political, religious, and social norms. Books with anthropomorphic animals have been documented as banned as early as the start of the 20th century, with books being banned for including content that is deemed socially, politically, or religiously unacceptable. A specific example of this is *The Call of the Wild* by Jack London, which depicts the story of a dog named Buck who rejects domestication to lead a pack of wolves. The book, published in 1903, was notably banned in Nazi Germany as it was seen as a call to rebel against authority ([Baldassarro](https://bbark.deepforestproductions.com/column/2011/07/24/banned-books-awareness-call-wild)).

Today, books that feature anthropomorphic animals are still challenged, particularly in school districts across the United States. However, in modern days, they are challenged not because of their fantastical elements themselves, but because of the political or philosophical ideas that might be embedded within them. Anthropomorphic animals can be used as a tool to project a controversial idea, such as political viewpoints or challenges to authority, rather than explicit content. *Animal Farm*, for example, has been restricted in some school districts for its political themes and critiques of government systems. Orwell represents a political revolution as an animal rebellion against human farmers. Using animals may allow authors to address controversial topics without directly pointing them out or putting themselves on the pedestal. It also may make the societal tension and power structures more universally accessible, therefore easier for even the younger readers to understand. This may explain why anthropomorphic literature continues to appear in censorship debates today.

There are also religious objections to novels featuring animals with human-like traits. In some religions that believe humans are distinct from other beings and created in the image of God, attributing human-like traits to animals puts animals on the same level as humans, which goes against their beliefs. For example, in 2006, a Kansas school district banned *Charlotte's Web* after parents pushed for the ban, calling it "unnatural and blasphemous as humans are the highest level of God's creation" ([Bush](https://readingpartners.org/blog/five-more-childrens-books-you-didnt-know-were-banned/)). Although book bans such as this have since been re-evaluated, there are still other similar titles that have been banned on religious, social, or political grounds.
