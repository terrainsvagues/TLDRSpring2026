## Book Banning in American Schools

<sup>Thilak, David, Jenny, Michelle</sup>

### Supreme Court Foundation

What content is appropriate for those under 18 to see? In 1982, the Supreme Court case *Island Trees Union Free School District v. Pico* gave school districts the authority to determine the content of their school libraries. It also laid out restrictions on what serves as proper motivation for a book ban. These restrictions ensure that the district cannot ban books purely due to political reasons (*Island Trees Sch. Dist. v. Pico*). However, restrictive school districts have maneuvered around their reasoning, attempting to justify bans that come from political reasons.

### Contemporary Examples

For example, [*The Family Book*](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/02group4.html) by Todd Parr was a children's picture book that was banned in 2022 in Lee County, Florida, the official reason stating "inclusion of same-sex couples." This ban has been challenged repeatedly using this SCOTUS case, although they have not made much progress. [*All Boys Aren't Blue*](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/07group7.html) by George M. Johnson explores the area of growing up Black and queer in America, and has been removed or restricted in multiple US school districts. School districts banned this book mainly because challengers labeled its candid discussion of sexual assault and its LGBTQ+ themes as "sexually explicit" and not age appropriate. Overall, while there are protections offered to books in school districts, they are not always upheld.

### Historical Context

Schools have banned books since at least the 1900s for a variety of reasons that went against the ideals of their society at the time. For example, [*Alice in Wonderland*](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/05group9.html) was banned in 1900 for disrespecting authority figures (Riordan). Tennessee banned the teaching of evolution in classrooms, including Charles Darwin's *Origin of Species*, from 1925 to 1967 ("Bannings and Burnings in History - Freedom to Read"). According to the American Library Association, schools and school libraries led the majority of book banning initiatives from 1990-1999, primarily for containing offensive language, sexually explicit content, and being unsuited for their age groups. Of the titles that were challenged, 3,421 were from parents, while 913 were from board members, administrators, and teachers combined, showing how the majority of historical book challenges have been from parents rather than administration.

### Current Trends

Book bannings in schools remain prevalent, mostly for including LGBTQ+ content and sexually explicit content. In 2024, the American Library Association recorded that of the 804 known institutions challenging books, 43% occurred in school libraries or schools. In the last 20 years, the percentage of total titles targeted by pressure groups and decision makers rose from 6% in 2005 to 72% in 2024 ([Top 10 Most Challenged Books of 2024 | Banned Books](https://www.ala.org/bbooks/frequentlychallengedbooks/top10)). Last year, the Trump administration "removed almost 600 books from Department of Defense Education Activity (DoDEA) schools" ([Flannery](https://www.nea.org/nea-today/all-news-articles/book-bans-are-common-and-rampant-so-are-educators-and-parents-fighting-them)), marking a new facet of involvement from the federal government in school book bannings. At the same time, 80% of people are opposed to federal and state governments' involvement in deciding which titles are available to students. There are many examples of successful lawsuits against book bannings and numerous state governments have started to pass "Freedom to Read" laws, showing how this process is in a continuous state of approval and disapproval.
