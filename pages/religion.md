## Banning Due to Religious Content ##

<sup> Alexander, Hannah, Leeza, Ryan </sup>

### Religious Heresy and Book Banning

Religious heresy has been a central theme across book banning around the world, with federal governments, institutions, school districts, and others restricting texts deemed "blasphemous," "immoral," or against the religious doctrine of their country. Within our own research, a notable example is J.K. Rowling's *[Harry Potter]([url](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/04group8.html))* series, banned in Tennessee, USA (2001) for "occult themes" by some Christian and Catholic groups for fear of influencing children toward "witchcraft." According to the American Library Association (ALA), the series was among the most frequently challenged books of 2001 and 2002, reflecting a nationwide (and perhaps global) trend of book banning based on religious heresy.

Religion has also been a common scapegoat in attempts to control content, effectively used as a cover for broader ideological restrictions in literature. While many challenges cite religious objections to themes like sexually explicit content or "non-traditional" perspectives, the deeper intent behind these bans is often to limit access to diverse and/or minority voices, particularly in school libraries or educational settings. Ultimately, while some objections are rooted in sincere faith, religion is frequently used as a legitimizing justification for what is, in effect, ideological censorship.

### Historical Roots of Religious Book Bans

Religion has been a common reason for book bans throughout history. Religious authorities often declared certain books heretical as a means of controlling the spread of ideas. They justified such bans by stating:

> "It is not permitted to read anything that is not included in the canon of Holy Scripture. If any have been written or are still being written, we reject them completely with disgust, as hurtful and entangling to the souls of simpler folk."

As early as the third century, the *Didascalia Apostolorum*, a Church order, instructed people to read nothing except the Bible. This ban helped to solidify the primacy of the Bible as the main source of knowledge. The process of keeping God's words as the only source of wisdom sometimes reached extremes, such as the public burning of books during the Bonfire of the Vanities in 1497.

One of the most significant moments in the history of Church control over literature was the *Index Librorum Prohibitorum* — an official list of books banned by the Catholic Church during the Protestant Reformation. The list was revised over time and its final version included works by famous authors such as Virginia Woolf, Albert Camus, and Jean-Paul Sartre. The list continued to grow until 1966, and according to religious authorities, the books on it were still considered dangerous to the Catholic faith.

### The Turn Toward Legal Protections

In more recent years, namely in the 1980s, people themselves sought to restrict children's access to books considered to be anti-Christian. This led to the first Supreme Court case that considered the right to receive information in a library; the court's decision cited the First Amendment and declared that institutions cannot restrict students' access to literature solely on the basis of personal dislike.

### Modern Religious Book Bans

Modern book bans largely reflect the historical pattern of religious book banning. Modern bans aim to consolidate authority within society by suppressing specific political views or redefining certain narratives as a potential moral threat.

*[The Satanic Verses]([url](https://terrainsvagues.github.io/TLDRSpring2026/items/07group2.html))*, banned by Iran in 1989, serves as a powerful example. Shortly after Rushdie published the novel, Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Khomeini issued a fatwa calling for his death. Iranian authorities condemned the book as blasphemy — an extreme insult to the Islamic religion. Among many reasons, one was that "two prostitutes in the book being given names of wives of the Prophet Muhammad" (BBC News). Beyond religious outrage, the fatwa also functioned as a means of political control. At the time, Iran was on the edge of going to war with Iraq while facing complex internal political tensions. The condemnation of Rushdie allowed the regime to position itself as the defender of Islamic values, justifying its initiatives both internally and on the global stage. In this way, the ban of *The Satanic Verses* is more than a religious issue — it is a political instrument that reflects the function of modern book banning.

### Conclusion

A key player in the growth of book banning has historically been religious distaste towards potentially "blasphemous" or "immoral" books. Instances such as the *Index Librorum Prohibitorum*, which only ended 60 years ago, and the banning of *The Satanic Verses* less than 40 years ago truly highlight how dominant religious institutions are in influencing censorship across entire countries. The unfortunate consequence of religion-related sensitivity towards literature is that important authors we now proudly celebrate experienced heavy persecution and were hindered from further producing and spreading their work.

As times change and books become more accessible than ever, a central question remains: what literature and sensitive topics does our society currently sanitize and hide from the public? History has demonstrated that these topics are equally important and that it is up to us to bring them to light.
