## Banning Due to Violent Content ##

<sup> Sera, Shelby, Pranav, Ivy </sup>

### Violence-Based Censorship

When violence is cited as the primary reason for a book ban, it is usually framed as a measure to protect immature minds of youth from moral corruption. This concern typically centers on the perceived traumatic impact of such content: an anxiety that is heightened when a narrative features young protagonists who are either subjected to or actively engaged in brutality. Ultimately, these challenges are driven by parental concern over their children's exposure to violence in any form. Specifically, it's noted in books like *The Hunger Games* and *The Hate U Give*, both of which have youth as the protagonists. We believe that due to teens having much of their traumatic experiences at that time of life, much violence-imbued literature is written from the perspective of a teen or young adult. However, engaging with the issue from a critical perspective, the American Library Association argues that labeling a book as "too violent" is often used to suppress works that challenge social norms or force readers to confront uncomfortable political truths ([American Library Association](https://www.ala.org/bbooks/frequentlychallengedbooks/top10)).

### Historical Context

Historically, books that included violence were banned by governments in order to prevent rebellion, uphold moral standards, and protect the youth from corruption. Governments were afraid that books portraying citizens turning against government control would inspire their people to do the same. One specific example is *Nineteen Eighty-Four* by George Orwell: it was criticized in the United States for its anti-government themes and violent, rebellious nature. Violence also threatened moral purity, which historically society fought to uphold. *Of Mice and Men* by John Steinbeck (1937) received backlash for its themes of violence and profanity being deemed "inappropriate" for students. Wanting to protect the youth's innocence, stories that desensitized children to strong themes were challenged. As a result, violent books are a major target when adults and parents try to protect children from violence in media like books. Books such as the *Goosebumps* Series (1990s) and *Harry Potter* Series (2000s) were banned in schools for their themes of violence and fear, despite children and teens being the target audience.

### Contemporary Concerns

Due to the recent increases in school shootings in the U.S., modern-day book bans have continued to target content that exposes youth communities to strong themes of violence, especially those depicting school violence. In particular, there was more scrutiny for any form of media entertainment, including books, that contained dark and violent themes. This applies most to the time period following the 1999 Columbine school shooting in Denver, Colorado ([EBSCO](www.ebsco.com/research-starters/literature-and-writing/censorship-banned-books)), which was a student-shooting that took place in a high school library. Additionally, modern day book bans have also targeted content depicting racism and sexual assault under the premise of removing "obscene" or "divisive" books. However, many people have argued that banning such books shuts down any discussion for improvement or change, and people may begin to turn a blind eye to our truthfully violent history.
