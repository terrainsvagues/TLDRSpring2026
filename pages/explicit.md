## Banning Due to Sexually Explicit Content ##

<sup> Jack, Arlene, Vee, Nicole </sup>

Sexually explicit content is the largest reason cited for book bannings or challenges, with these bannings frequently occurring at the K-12 grade levels within the United States. A large concern with sexually explicit content, as voiced by book challengers such as parents and organized groups, is that said “obscene” content threatens to be harmful to minors or influential to impressionable minds. Regardless of the severity of the content, or how relevant its other narrative contents may be, a large sum of books appear to be grouped and targeted under the guise of removing sexually explicit content. Furthermore, the sexually explicit label is frequently misused and weaponized in order to justify the removal of controversial books regardless of the presence of truly sexually graphic material. Some relevant examples throughout American history include _The Handmaid’s Tale_, _1984_, and _Brave New World_, all prominent and appraised novels within their own rights, yet which still met bans and challenges under the guise of protecting minors. 

Books have been banned for sexual obscenity, immorality, and the intention of protecting the younger audience since the early 1800s, with banning rates rising during historical social movements. In 1873, bans against sexually explicit texts were enacted through the Comstock Laws that prohibited the distribution of “indecent” material through mail. This was revised in 1957 with Roth v. United States to only concern texts that were “utterly without redeeming social importance”. For many decades, legal battles have debated on what books should be allowed. For example, the 1973 Miller v. California Supreme Court case’s ruling defined what materials should be allowed to be distributed, and whether freedom of speech guarantees a person’s right to distribute “obscene materials” ([Oyez](www.oyez.org/cases/1971/70-73)). The early 20th century has seen a rise in groups like the New England Watch and Ward Society (National Geographic), and parent groups that target books containing explicit language and sexual content. However, in recent years, “sexually explicit content” has been used as a reason to challenge LGBTQ+ novels in Young Adult literature. 

In modern times, book bans frequently cite “sexually explicit” content as their primary justification. As of 2020, 92.5% of books on the list were challenged or banned due to this reason ([First Amendment Museum](firstamendmentmuseum.org/how-do-books-get-banned/)). Additionally, for all this rhetoric about explicit books, research has found that only 13% of banned titles had actual “on-page” sexual descriptions ([Pen America](pen.org/report/cover-to-cover/)). Others were challenged for simply referencing anatomy or relationships, especially in the realm of LGBTQ+ characters. 67% of banned books are specifically intended for young adult audiences; even if books don’t have “on-page” explicit content, tagging them with this content makes it easier for administrators to control what children are reading as they mature and develop their own perspectives in life. Ultimately, the theme of sexually explicit content in book banning has evolved through history, most prominently appearing in the 1800s and continuing to persist in the modern world.
